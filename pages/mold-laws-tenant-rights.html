Mold Laws & Tenant Rights

What the Law Actually Says — and What You Can Do About Mold in a Rental

Mold in a rental property creates a complicated legal and emotional situation. Most tenants don't know their rights. Most landlords don't know their obligations. And the law varies significantly by state. This page gives you the framework to understand where you stand.

There is no federal standard for acceptable mold levels in residential properties. The EPA does not regulate mold in homes. This means mold law is primarily a state and local issue — and most states don't have specific mold statutes either.

However, most states do have implied warranty of habitability requirements, which means landlords must maintain rental properties in a livable condition. Significant mold problems — especially those caused by water intrusion the landlord failed to repair — can constitute a habitability violation.

  1. Document everything — Photos, dates, correspondence. Written communication (email, certified mail) is better than verbal complaints.
  2. Report in writing — Notify the landlord of the mold problem in writing. Keep a copy. Many states require written notice before any legal action.
  3. Request inspection and repair — Ask the landlord to inspect, identify the moisture source, and remediate properly.
  4. Contact local code enforcement or health department — They may inspect and issue violations if habitability standards are not met.
  5. Know your state's remedies — Depending on the state, options may include rent withholding, repair-and-deduct, lease termination, or legal action.
  • Withhold rent without understanding your state's rules — Some states allow it under specific conditions. Others don't. Doing it wrong can get you evicted.
  • Remove or remediate mold yourself in a rental — This can damage the landlord's property and shift liability to you. It can also eliminate evidence of the problem.
  • Move out without following proper procedures — If you break the lease without following your state's constructive eviction process, you may owe remaining rent.

A few states have more specific mold-related statutes or regulations:

  • California — Toxic Mold Protection Act (2001). Requires disclosure and has assessment standards.
  • Texas — Mold Assessment and Remediation Rules. Requires licensing for assessors and remediators.
  • New York — NYC Local Law 55 requires landlords to investigate and remediate mold.
  • Indiana, Maryland, New Jersey, Virginia — Various disclosure or remediation requirements.

In any tenant-landlord mold dispute, documentation is your strongest tool:

  • Dated photos and videos of the mold, water damage, and affected areas
  • Written communication — every report, request, and response should be in writing
  • Medical records if you're experiencing health symptoms
  • Independent inspection reports (if you've hired an assessor)
  • Records of all landlord responses (or lack thereof)
