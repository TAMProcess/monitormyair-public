What Good Remediation Looks Like

How to Judge the Work — Before, During, and After

Most homeowners have no frame of reference for what professional mold remediation should look like. This page shows you what proper work includes, what to watch for during the process, and how to verify the job was done right.

  • A written remediation plan or scope of work is provided
  • The moisture source has been identified and either fixed or included in the scope
  • Pre-remediation air testing has been done (ideally by an independent assessor)
  • The contractor has explained containment, removal, and clearance procedures
  • You understand what will be removed, what will be cleaned, and how success is measured

Professional remediation should include:

  • Containment — Plastic barriers seal the work area from the rest of the building. You shouldn't smell chemicals or see dust outside the containment zone.
  • Negative air pressure — Air scrubbers with HEPA filters run continuously. The work area should be under negative pressure (air flows in, not out).
  • Worker protection — Workers wear PPE: respirators (not just dust masks), Tyvek suits, and gloves.
  • Proper removal — Contaminated drywall, insulation, and porous materials are cut out (not scrubbed), double-bagged, and removed through a designated exit path.
  • HEPA vacuuming and damp wiping — After material removal, all remaining surfaces in the containment zone are HEPA vacuumed and damp-wiped.
  • No one living in the work zone — The containment area should not be occupied during remediation.

Clearance is the verification step that confirms the remediation was successful:

  • Visual inspection — No visible mold, debris, or dust remaining. Surfaces are clean.
  • Moisture readings — Affected materials should read dry. If they don't, the moisture source hasn't been resolved.
  • Air sampling — Post-remediation air samples compared to pre-remediation baselines and outdoor controls. Results should show a significant reduction.
  • Independent clearance — The person performing clearance should NOT be the same company that performed the remediation. Independence is essential.
  • No containment barriers set up
  • Workers not wearing proper PPE
  • Spraying chemicals without removing contaminated materials
  • Painting or encapsulating over moldy surfaces
  • No air scrubbers or HEPA equipment visible
  • No post-remediation clearance testing performed
  • The same company doing remediation and clearance
  • Musty smell remains after the job is "complete"
  • IICRC S520 — Standard and Reference Guide for Professional Mold Remediation. The most widely recognized industry standard.
  • ANSI/IICRC S500 — Standard for water damage restoration (since water damage precedes mold).
  • EPA Mold Remediation in Schools and Commercial Buildings — A free, publicly available guide that outlines proper procedures.

Ask your contractor if they follow IICRC standards. If they don't know what that means — keep looking.
