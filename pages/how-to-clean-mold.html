How to Clean Mold

What Works, What Doesn't, and When Cleaning Isn't Enough

Everyone wants a simple answer: "What do I use to clean mold?" The real answer is: it depends on the surface, the size of the problem, and whether the underlying cause has been addressed. This page gives you a framework for making good cleaning decisions — not just product recommendations.

Non-porous surfaces (tile, glass, metal, sealed countertops) — Mold sits on the surface and can usually be effectively cleaned.

Semi-porous surfaces (wood, concrete) — Mold may penetrate the surface. Light contamination can sometimes be cleaned; heavy contamination may require sanding, blasting, or replacement.

Porous surfaces (drywall, carpet, insulation, ceiling tiles) — These materials absorb moisture and mold grows into them. Cleaning the surface doesn't reach the growth inside the material. In most cases, contaminated porous materials should be removed, not cleaned.

Bleach is the most commonly recommended mold cleaner — and one of the least effective for most mold situations:

  • Bleach works on non-porous surfaces but doesn't penetrate porous materials
  • On drywall or wood, bleach whitens the surface (making mold invisible) while the roots remain
  • Bleach adds water to the surface — which can feed remaining mold
  • Bleach fumes in enclosed spaces can cause respiratory irritation

The EPA no longer recommends bleach for routine mold cleaning. Detergent and water, or hydrogen peroxide solutions, are generally more appropriate.

You can generally handle mold cleaning yourself when:

  • The affected area is less than 10 square feet (roughly 3' × 3')
  • Mold is on a non-porous surface
  • You know the moisture source and it's been fixed
  • You don't have existing respiratory conditions
  • There's no musty smell suggesting hidden mold behind the surface

Basic cleaning protocol:

  1. Wear an N95 mask, gloves, and eye protection
  2. Don't dry-scrub or sweep (this releases spores)
  3. Wet the area first
  4. Use detergent and water, or hydrogen peroxide
  5. Scrub the surface, then wipe clean
  6. Ensure the area dries completely
  • The affected area exceeds 10 square feet
  • Mold is on drywall, insulation, or other porous materials
  • You can smell mold but can't see it (hidden mold)
  • Mold is in the HVAC system
  • The moisture source hasn't been identified or fixed
  • You or household members have health symptoms
  • You're a tenant and the landlord is responsible for the property
  • Ozone generators — Marketed as mold killers, but ozone at effective concentrations is harmful to people, pets, and building materials. Not recommended by the EPA for mold.
  • "Mold-proof" paints applied over mold — Encapsulating mold without removing it traps moisture and doesn't solve the problem.
  • Foggers and aerosol treatments — May kill surface mold temporarily but don't address root causes or contaminated materials.
