Clear Answers to Common Mold & Air Quality Questions

About Mold

No. Mold is everywhere — outdoors and indoors. The concern is when mold grows indoors in large quantities, especially in occupied living spaces. Some mold species produce mycotoxins that can cause health effects, and even non-toxic molds can trigger allergies and respiratory irritation. The issue isn't whether mold exists — it's whether it's growing where it shouldn't be, in quantities that affect your health or your building.

Moisture. That's it. Mold needs moisture, a food source (any organic material — drywall, wood, carpet, dust), and time. It can begin growing within 24–48 hours of a surface getting wet. The root cause is always a moisture problem: leaks, condensation, high humidity, flooding, or inadequate ventilation.

Bleach is not recommended for most mold cleaning. It works on non-porous surfaces like tile and glass, but it does not penetrate porous materials like drywall or wood — which is where most mold grows. The EPA, IICRC, and most professional standards no longer recommend bleach for mold remediation. Better options include hydrogen peroxide, vinegar, or commercial mold-specific products. Most importantly, you must fix the moisture source or the mold will return regardless of what you clean it with.

Mold can begin growing within 24 to 48 hours after a surface becomes wet — if it stays wet. This is why rapid drying after any water event is critical. If materials can be dried within 24–48 hours, mold growth can usually be prevented. After 48 hours, the risk increases significantly.

About Health

Yes. Mold exposure can cause a range of health effects including respiratory symptoms (coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath), nasal congestion, eye irritation, skin rashes, headaches, fatigue, and cognitive difficulties. People with asthma, allergies, immune deficiencies, or chronic lung conditions are at higher risk. Some mold species produce mycotoxins that can cause more serious health effects with prolonged exposure. See our Mold Exposure Symptoms page for a comprehensive list.

If you're experiencing symptoms that started or worsened after moving into a building, or that improve when you leave the building, consider seeing a doctor — ideally one experienced with environmental illness. Not all doctors are familiar with mold-related health effects. See our Find a Mold-Literate Doctor page for guidance on finding the right kind of physician.

The term "black mold" usually refers to Stachybotrys chartarum, which can produce mycotoxins. However, mold color is not a reliable indicator of danger. Many harmless molds are black, and many harmful molds are not. The species, the quantity, the duration of exposure, and your individual sensitivity all matter more than the color. Don't panic about color — but don't ignore any indoor mold growth.

About Inspections & Remediation

Large-scale remediation involving multiple rooms, HVAC systems, or structural components can range from $5,000 to $30,000+. The most important factor is not the price — it's whether the scope of work actually addresses the moisture source and follows proper remediation standards.
