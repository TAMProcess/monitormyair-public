Remediation Plans

What a Real Plan Looks Like — and Why You Need One Before Work Starts

A remediation plan is the written scope of work that defines what will be done, how it will be done, and how success will be measured. Without one, you're paying someone to do something with no agreed-upon standard for what "done" means.

Most mold remediation disputes happen because there was no written plan — or the plan was vague. A proper remediation plan:

  • Defines the scope of work (what areas, what materials)
  • Specifies containment and worker protection requirements
  • Details how affected materials will be removed and disposed of
  • Describes how the moisture source will be addressed
  • Establishes clearance criteria — how you'll know the job is done

If a contractor says "we'll take care of it" without providing a written plan — that's a red flag.

  1. Scope of affected areas — Specific rooms, wall sections, materials, and square footage
  2. Containment plan — How the work area will be isolated (plastic barriers, negative air pressure, air scrubbers)
  3. Material removal plan — What's being removed (drywall, insulation, carpet, etc.) and to what extent
  4. Moisture source remediation — How the underlying water problem will be fixed (or confirmation that it's already fixed)
  5. Cleaning methods — HEPA vacuuming, damp wiping, antimicrobial treatment on appropriate surfaces
  6. Waste disposal — How contaminated materials will be bagged, transported, and disposed of
  7. Clearance testing — Who will perform post-remediation verification, what testing will be done, and what criteria define success

Ideally, the remediation plan should be written by — or at least reviewed by — someone independent of the remediation contractor. This is a fundamental principle of good mold remediation:

  • The person who identifies the problem should not be the same person who profits from fixing it
  • An independent industrial hygienist (IH) or mold assessor can write the plan based on inspection findings
  • The contractor then bids on and executes the plan
  • The same IH or assessor performs clearance testing after the work is done

This three-party model (assessor → contractor → verifier) is the industry standard for protecting consumers.

  • No written scope of work
  • Vague language like "treat all areas" without specifics
  • No mention of containment
  • No plan for addressing the moisture source
  • No clearance testing included
  • The same company that found the mold is proposing to fix it — and verify their own work
  • Pressure to start immediately without allowing time for review
  • Template for evaluating remediation proposals
  • Checklist of what should be included in any plan
  • Contractor vetting criteria and interview questions
  • Understanding industry standards (IICRC S520, ANSI/ACAC)
  • Expert review of your specific remediation proposal
