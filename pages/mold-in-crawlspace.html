Mold in the Crawlspace

What's Below Your Floor Affects Everything Above It

Crawlspaces are one of the most common — and most misunderstood — sources of mold problems in homes. Because you can't see it from the living space, crawlspace mold often goes unnoticed for years. But building science is clear: air from the crawlspace enters the home. If there's mold below, it's affecting the air above.

The stack effect pulls air upward through the home — from the crawlspace, through the floors, and out through the upper levels. Studies show that 40–60% of the air you breathe on the first floor originates from the crawlspace.

  • Exposed earth releases moisture constantly
  • Poor drainage and grading push water toward foundations
  • Open vents (once recommended) allow humid outdoor air in
  • Plumbing leaks go undetected for months
  • Wood framing, insulation, and debris provide food for mold
  • Musty smell on the first floor, especially near interior walls or closets
  • Sagging or soft floors
  • High indoor humidity despite running AC
  • Visible mold on floor joists, subfloor, or sill plates
  • Standing water or damp soil in the crawlspace
  • Condensation on ductwork or pipes in the crawlspace

Vented crawlspaces (with open foundation vents) were once standard practice. Research now shows that in most climates, vented crawlspaces make moisture problems worse — especially in the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic regions. Warm, humid outdoor air enters, condenses on cooler surfaces, and feeds mold growth.

Encapsulated crawlspaces use vapor barriers, sealed vents, and sometimes dehumidifiers to control moisture. This approach is now considered best practice by building scientists and is increasingly required by updated building codes.

  • Spraying mold kill products — Surface treatments on wood framing are temporary. If the moisture source isn't fixed, mold returns.
  • Adding more vents — More ventilation often increases moisture in humid climates. Counterintuitive, but well-documented.
  • Laying plastic without sealing — A loose sheet of plastic on the ground is not encapsulation. Proper encapsulation involves sealed edges, taped seams, and moisture management.
  • Ignoring the crawlspace because "it's not living space" — Building science doesn't care about room labels. Air moves through the entire structure.
  1. Address bulk water first — Fix grading, gutters, and drainage before anything else.
  2. Identify and fix plumbing leaks
  3. Remediate existing mold on structural wood — Soda blasting, dry ice blasting, or HEPA sanding depending on severity.
  4. Encapsulate the crawlspace — Vapor barrier, sealed vents, and dehumidification.
  5. Verify results — Post-remediation testing to confirm effectiveness.
