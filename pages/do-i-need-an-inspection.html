Do I Need a Mold Inspection?

When Testing Makes Sense — and When It's a Waste of Money

Not every mold situation requires professional testing. Some do. The difference matters — because bad advice here can cost you hundreds (or thousands) of dollars, or leave a serious problem unaddressed. Here's how to decide.

  • You can see the mold — Visible mold growth doesn't need to be "confirmed" by a lab. You already know it's there. Focus on fixing the moisture source and cleaning it properly.
  • Small area (under 10 sq. ft.) — For small patches in bathrooms or on window sills, proper cleaning and moisture control is usually sufficient without testing.
  • You already know the moisture source — If the cause is obvious (e.g., a bathroom with no exhaust fan), fix the source and clean the mold.

Testing visible mold just to confirm it's mold is usually unnecessary and a waste of money. If you can see it, deal with it.

  • You smell mold but can't see it — Hidden mold behind walls, under floors, or in HVAC systems requires professional assessment to locate
  • You're experiencing unexplained health symptoms — Especially if symptoms improve when you leave the building and return when you come back
  • You're buying or renting a property — Pre-purchase or pre-lease inspection can prevent costly problems
  • After water damage or flooding — Even after cleanup, mold can develop in hidden areas within 24–48 hours
  • You need documentation for legal purposes — Tenant disputes, insurance claims, or real estate transactions require professional lab reports
  • Post-remediation verification — Independent clearance testing after professional remediation to confirm the job was done right
  • Large affected area (over 10 sq. ft.) — Professional assessment should precede remediation for larger problems
  • Air sampling — Captures airborne mold spores. Useful for comparing indoor vs. outdoor levels and detecting hidden mold. Most common type.
  • Surface sampling — Tape lift, swab, or bulk sample from a visible surface. Identifies the type of mold on a specific material.
  • Bulk sampling — A piece of material is sent to a lab. Most accurate for identifying mold type and extent of contamination within building materials.
  • ERMI / HERTSMI-2 — Dust-based DNA analysis. Provides a comprehensive mold profile. Popular with mold-literate doctors for health-focused assessments.

The right test depends on your situation. The Library's decision tools help you determine which type (if any) you actually need.

  • Independence — The inspector should NOT also do remediation. This is a conflict of interest. The person who finds the problem should not be the same person who profits from fixing it.
  • Credentials — Look for certifications from ACAC (American Council for Accredited Certification), IICRC, or state-specific licensing
  • Experience — Ask how many inspections they've done. Ask for references. Ask about their protocol.
  • Lab partnership — They should use an accredited lab (AIHA EMLAP accredited) for sample analysis
  • Written report — You should receive a detailed written report with findings, photos, lab results, and recommendations
  1. Visual assessment — Thorough walkthrough of the entire property, including attic, crawlspace, and HVAC
  2. Moisture mapping — Using moisture meters and thermal imaging to identify hidden moisture
  3. Air and/or surface sampling — Based on the situation; indoor vs. outdoor comparison samples
  4. Lab analysis — Samples sent to an accredited laboratory for identification and quantification
  5. Written report — Findings, lab results, photos, moisture readings, and actionable recommendations
  6. Recommendations — Whether remediation is needed, scope of work, and priority level

  • They diagnose "toxic mold" based only on visual inspection
  • They recommend extensive remediation before any testing
  • They use fear tactics or urgency to sell services
  • They can't show credentials or name the lab they use
  • They don't include outdoor control samples in air testing
  • They offer a price that seems too good to be true (often means it is)
