Mold in Your HVAC System

If It's in Your Ducts, It's in Your Air

Your HVAC system moves air through every room of your home. If mold is growing inside the system — in the air handler, on the coils, in the ductwork — it's being distributed throughout the building every time the system runs.

This makes HVAC mold one of the most impactful and most overlooked sources of indoor mold exposure.

  • Condensation on the evaporator coil — The coil is cold and wet by design. If the drain pan or drain line is clogged, or the system doesn't dehumidify properly, standing water provides the moisture mold needs.
  • Duct leaks in unconditioned spaces — Ducts running through hot attics or humid crawlspaces can develop condensation inside, creating perfect mold conditions.
  • High indoor humidity — If the home is consistently above 60% relative humidity, moisture condenses inside ductwork.
  • Dirty filters and organic debris — Dust and organic material trapped in the system become food for mold.
  • Post-flooding or water damage — If the HVAC system operated during or after a water event, contaminated air may have been pulled through the system.
  • Musty or stale smell when the system runs
  • Visible mold around vents, registers, or the air handler
  • Dark discoloration on duct insulation
  • Symptoms that worsen when the system turns on
  • Condensation or water stains near vents

If the smell comes specifically when the AC or heat kicks on — and goes away when the system is off — the HVAC system is a strong suspect.

The duct cleaning industry is largely unregulated, and many companies use aggressive marketing that doesn't match the science. Standard duct cleaning may remove loose debris but won't:

  • Fix the moisture source causing mold growth
  • Clean the evaporator coil, drain pan, or blower assembly
  • Replace contaminated duct insulation
  • Address duct leaks in unconditioned spaces

Without addressing the root cause, mold will return — often within weeks.

  1. Identify the moisture source — Is it condensation, a clogged drain, duct leaks, or high indoor humidity?
  2. Inspect the air handler — The coil, drain pan, blower, and cabinet are the most common locations for HVAC mold.
  3. Evaluate duct condition — Flex duct with insulation liner is extremely difficult to clean and may need replacement if contaminated.
  4. Fix the problem, then clean — Address the moisture issue first. Then clean or replace affected components. Otherwise you're cleaning something that will just get dirty again.
  5. Verify with testing — Air testing before and after can help confirm whether the remediation was effective.
