Find a Remediation Contractor

Not All Contractors Are Equal — Here's How to Find a Good One

Hiring the wrong mold remediation contractor can cost you thousands of dollars and leave you with the same problem — or worse. The mold remediation industry is largely unregulated, which means the burden of vetting falls on you.

  • IICRC certification — Specifically in mold remediation (AMRT) and/or water damage restoration (WRT)
  • Insurance — General liability and pollution liability insurance. Ask for certificates.
  • Written scope of work — A good contractor provides a detailed remediation plan, not just a price.
  • Willingness to work with an independent assessor — They should welcome independent oversight, not resist it.
  • References and experience — Ask for recent project references. Look for experience with situations similar to yours.
  • Clear communication — They should explain their process, answer your questions, and be transparent about what they will and won't do.
  • "Free mold testing" — This often means they'll find mold (surprise) and then pressure you to hire them for remediation. Independent testing is essential.
  • No written scope of work or remediation plan
  • Guaranteeing mold will never come back — No one can guarantee this. Honest contractors manage expectations.
  • Refusing to allow independent clearance testing
  • High-pressure sales tactics or urgency ("this is an emergency — sign today")
  • Using scare tactics about health effects to close a sale
  • The same company performing testing, remediation, AND clearance
  1. What certifications do you hold? (Look for IICRC AMRT/WRT)
  2. Do you carry pollution liability insurance?
  3. Will you provide a written remediation plan or scope of work?
  4. How do you handle containment and worker protection?
  5. How is the moisture source being addressed in your plan?
  6. Will you remove contaminated materials or just treat surfaces?
  7. Who performs clearance testing — you or an independent party?
  8. Can you provide references from recent projects?

The industry best practice for consumer protection:

  1. Independent Assessor/IH — Identifies the problem, writes the remediation plan
  2. Remediation Contractor — Executes the plan
  3. Independent Clearance Tester — Verifies the work was done correctly (often the same assessor from step 1)

When the same company does all three, there's a built-in conflict of interest. Separation protects you.
