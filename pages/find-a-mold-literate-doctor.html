Find a Mold-Literate Doctor

Most Doctors Aren't Trained in This. Here's How to Find One Who Is.

If you suspect mold is affecting your health, the single most impactful next step may be finding the right doctor — not just any doctor, but one who understands how environmental exposures affect the body.

This page explains what "mold-literate" means, why it matters, and how to find one.

A mold-literate doctor is a physician who understands that indoor environmental exposures — including mold, water-damaged buildings, and biotoxins — can cause or contribute to chronic illness. They don't dismiss environmental complaints, and they know which tests and frameworks to use for proper evaluation.

These doctors may specialize in environmental medicine, functional medicine, integrative medicine, or may be conventionally trained physicians who've added environmental health to their practice.

Most medical training doesn't include environmental health. Your primary care doctor or allergist may be excellent — but mold-related illness requires specialized knowledge about:

  • How biotoxins affect the immune and nervous systems
  • What lab markers to order (beyond standard panels)
  • How to differentiate environmental illness from autoimmune, psychiatric, or idiopathic diagnoses
  • When environmental investigation should precede or run alongside treatment
  • Detailed exposure history (water damage, building symptoms, timeline)
  • Multi-system symptom assessment
  • Specific lab panels (inflammatory markers, biotoxin markers, immune function)
  • Possible genetic testing (HLA-DR) to assess susceptibility
  • Environmental correlation — connecting health findings to building conditions

Not every mold-literate doctor uses the same framework, but the good ones connect your symptoms to your environment — not just your body in isolation.

Finding a qualified mold-literate doctor is one of the hardest parts of this process. Many don't advertise as such. Here's where to look:

  • ISEAI — International Society for Environmentally Acquired Illness: maintains a provider directory
  • IFM — Institute for Functional Medicine: search their practitioner network
  • Surviving Mold — Dr. Shoemaker's network of certified practitioners
  • ACEP — American College for Advancement in Medicine
  • The Decision Toolkit — our AI helps you locate relevant practitioners and prepare for your first visit

Beware of practitioners who:

  • Promise to "cure" mold illness with a single supplement or protocol
  • Diagnose without proper testing
  • Refuse to consider environmental factors
  • Sell expensive proprietary products as the primary treatment
  • Dismiss all conventional medicine

Good practitioners work with you, not on you. They use evidence, ask questions, and connect the dots between your symptoms, labs, and environment.
